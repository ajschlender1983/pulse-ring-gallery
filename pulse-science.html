1

Pulse

The pattern interrupt

Automatic habits run through the basal ganglia — low effort, no thought, the scroll that scrolls itself. A gentle, unexpected vibration on your finger fires the prefrontal cortex, the brain's conscious control center. The loop can't keep running unnoticed.

2

Pause

The loop breaks

Habits move in a loop: trigger → behavior → reward. The pulse lands mid-behavior and stops the pattern before it completes. For a moment, the automatic thing simply… doesn't happen.

3

Presence

The window opens

The unexpected shift releases a small dose of dopamine, easing the change of mental state and opening a brief window of neuroplasticity. You're back in the room — awake, aware, and free to choose.

4

Practice

The replacement rule

An interrupt stops the old habit; it doesn't build the new one. Each pulse, swap in one small chosen action — a breath, a look up, a hand reached out. Repetition writes the new pathway.

How long does rewiring take?

On average, a new behavior becomes automatic in about 66 days — ranging from 18 to 254 depending on complexity (Lally et al., European Journal of Social Psychology). That's why Pulse is worn, not opened: dozens of gentle interrupts a day, every day, until the new pattern is simply yours.
